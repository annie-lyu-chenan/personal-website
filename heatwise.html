*The project was developed in collaboration with Emily Yang, Olivia Ma, and Isa Chen from Boston University. The project was built on a prize-winning proposal from Megan Rydar at Boston University, who also contributed to the research and design procedures.

Problem

Environmental science education lacks an engaging and accessible digital learning tool that effectively supports both educators and students.

User Research

Target Audience

9th-12th grade students in Boston Public Schools and their environmental science teachers.

Methods

Focus groups and user interviews were carried out among 11th grade students and their environmental science teachers in Boston Green Academy.

User Interview Highlights

  • Students
    • Long lectures about science were overwhelming and disengaging for students.
    • Customization (for example, customizing the appearances of avatars), visualizations, and hands-on experiences could make students feel connected.
    • Learning gaps were found among students.
  • Educators
    • Emphasized engagement and progress over grades.
    • Extended learning curve of some existing tools (for example, story maps) had an impact on usability.
    • Students only have access to Chromebooks in classroom.

Design System

Challenges & Solutions

Tracking Community Forest Planting

Miyawaki Method was integrated into Boston Public Schools' environmental science curriculum, enabling students to observe self-sustaining forest ecosystems within their own communities.

A centralized data hub was developed to help teachers collect and visualize information related to the forests planted. The data hub functioned as both an engagement tool and an instructional resource.

The assignment submission interface was redesigned with structured input fields and feedback features to streamline the data collection process.

Enhancing Engagement among Students with Varied Knowledge Levels

Progress trackers were implemented in place of traditional grades, aligning with educators' goals of prioritizing student engagement over evaluation.

Customizable characters were adopted from Idori to create a more personalized and motivating experience through visual identity and companionship.

Usability Testing

Improvements

Applied more robust course navigation other than hovering to see the next level of content.

Future Directions

After completing our phase of user research and initial design, the project was handed off to a new UX team for continued refinement and expansion.

UI Improvements

The current interface featured basic visual design and limited branding consistency. Future iterations should prioritize a more unified and polished UI system, with cohesive visual elements and a clearer design language.

Student-End UX Development

While the current version focuses on the teacher-facing experience based on our user research findings, future iterations will shift toward the student end. Specifically, expanding customization options and enhancing visual feedback, such as using a growing digital tree of students' choice to track assignment progress.
